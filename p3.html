Why Study IS History?

Understanding the history of Information Systems is like understanding the human evolution‚Äîit helps us see how we got here and where we're heading. Each era brought major breakthroughs that fundamentally changed how businesses operate:

üìä The Business Impact

Companies that understand IS evolution make better technology investments. They avoid repeating past mistakes and capitalize on emerging opportunities faster.

üîÆ Future Prediction

2024 Reality: Every major business transformation traces back to an IS innovation. Netflix replaced Blockbuster (streaming tech). Uber replaced taxi systems (mobile + GPS). Understanding this history is crucial for your career in information systems.

The Six Eras of Information Systems

1
1950s-1960s

The Mainframe Era

‚öôÔ∏è The Technology

  • ‚Ä¢ Room-sized computers (IBM System/360)
  • ‚Ä¢ Punch cards for input
  • ‚Ä¢ Mainframe cost: $1-3 million (1960s)
  • ‚Ä¢ Computing limited to large corporations

üíº Business Impact

  • ‚úì Automated payroll processing
  • ‚úì First financial systems
  • ‚úì Centralized data management
  • ‚úì Created "DP Departments"
  • ‚úì Required formal training

üì∞ Real Example: NASA used IBM mainframes for the Apollo moon landing calculations (1960s). A single calculation took hours on a mainframe‚Äîtoday's smartphones are 1 million+ times faster!

2
1970s-1980s

The Minicomputer Era

‚öôÔ∏è The Technology

  • ‚Ä¢ Refrigerator-sized computers
  • ‚Ä¢ DEC (Digital Equipment Corporation) PDP-11
  • ‚Ä¢ Cost: $100,000-300,000
  • ‚Ä¢ Departments could now own computers
  • ‚Ä¢ Unix operating system emerged

üíº Business Impact

  • ‚úì Decentralization of computing
  • ‚úì Manufacturing & engineering got own systems
  • ‚úì Real-time data processing
  • ‚úì First department-level IS investments
  • ‚úì Beginning of business unit IT

üì∞ Real Example: DEC's PDP-11 was so successful that Ken Olson (founder) famously said "There is no reason anyone would want a computer in their home" in 1977‚Äîjust before the PC revolution proved him spectacularly wrong!

‚öôÔ∏è The Technology

  • ‚Ä¢ Powerful enough for individual use

  • ‚úì Every employee got a computer
  • ‚úì "Power users" emerged in orgs

  • ‚úì End-user computing shifted power
  • ‚úì Created shadow IT phenomenon

üì∞ Real Example: Lotus 1-2-3 (spreadsheet software, 1983) was so useful that businesses bought IBM PCs just to run it. This single app drove PC adoption across industries‚Äîshowing how a killer app can transform technology adoption.

How IS Evolution Transformed Organizations

1 From Centralization to Decentralization

üìä Mainframe Era (1960s)

  • ‚úì One central IT department controlled all computing
  • ‚úì Business units depended entirely on IT
  • ‚úì IT was seen as "cost center"
  • ‚úì Business operations could take weeks to request changes
  • ‚úì IT had ALL the power in organization

üåê Cloud & Edge Era (2024)

  • ‚úì Business units buy their own cloud tools (Slack, Salesforce)
  • ‚úì No-code/low-code empowers business users
  • ‚úì IT is "enabler" supporting business innovation
  • ‚úì Changes deploy in minutes/seconds
  • ‚úì Business and IT work as partners

Impact: This shift changed organizational power dynamics. Companies moved from IT dictating what's possible to business needs driving technology choices. Today's organizations struggle with "shadow IT" where departments buy tools without IT approval.

2 From CapEx to OpEx

üí∞ Traditional Model (1980s-2000s)

Cost Model

Capital Expenditure (CapEx)

  • ‚Ä¢ Buy servers: $500,000 upfront
  • ‚Ä¢ Depreciate over 5 years
  • ‚Ä¢ Server becomes obsolete in 4 years
  • ‚Ä¢ Stuck with 1 year of outdated tech
  • ‚Ä¢ High financial risk

üîÑ Cloud Model (2024)

Cost Model

Operating Expenditure (OpEx)

  • ‚Ä¢ Pay $5,000/month for AWS
  • ‚Ä¢ Always use latest hardware
  • ‚Ä¢ Cancel anytime, no waste
  • ‚Ä¢ Low financial risk
  • ‚Ä¢ Scalability built-in

Impact: This shift democratized technology. Startups no longer needed millions in upfront capital to compete. You could start a global company from your laptop using AWS. This enabled the startup boom of the 2010s-2020s.

3 IT Staffing Evolution

üñ•Ô∏è Mainframe Era (1960s-1980s)

Companies had large IT departments: operators, programmers, data entry clerks, system administrators. IBM employed 400,000+ people at its peak.

üíª PC Era (1980s-1990s)

IT staffing exploded. Every company needed: network admins, help desk staff, system integrators. IT became major cost for businesses.

‚òÅÔ∏è Cloud Era (2010s-2024)

IT staffing DECREASED at traditional companies. AWS handles infrastructure. But NEW skills needed: data scientists, ML engineers, cloud architects. Massive talent shortage in these areas.

Employment Shift Impact

Declining Roles

System admins, data entry, help desk

Growing Roles

Data scientists, AI engineers, cloud architects

New Challenges

180,000+ unfilled tech jobs (US, 2024)

4 Speed to Market & Agility

‚è∞ 1980s: Mainframe

Dev Time

18-24 months

Business request to production: nearly 2 years

‚ö° 2000s: Internet

Dev Time

3-6 months

Faster, but still quarterly releases

üöÄ 2024: Cloud/DevOps

Dev Time

1 day

Continuous deployment: changes per hour/minute

Impact: Companies that deploy features fast win market share. Netflix can push 1,000+ updates per day. Traditional companies taking months to launch features get disrupted. This is why Blockbuster lost to Netflix, why taxis lost to Uber.

5 Global Operations & Remote Work

üè¢ Pre-Cloud Era

  • ‚úì Each office needed servers
  • ‚úì IT staff in every location
  • ‚úì Data stored locally
  • ‚úì Remote access nearly impossible
  • ‚úì Teams must be co-located

üåç Cloud Era (2024)

  • ‚úì One global cloud infrastructure
  • ‚úì Minimal IT staff needed
  • ‚úì Centralized, accessible data
  • ‚úì Remote work seamless
  • ‚úì Teams distributed across countries

COVID-19 Lesson: Companies with cloud-based systems smoothly transitioned to remote work in 2020. Companies with legacy on-premise systems struggled. This accelerated cloud adoption by an estimated 5 years globally.

Group Activity

Group Discussion: "What If?" Scenario

Format: In-class discussion | Duration: 30-40 minutes (Day 7)

üí≠
üìã Scenario-Based Discussion:

Your group will discuss a "What If?" scenario exploring how different IS decisions would have changed technology history.

Scenario A: What if IBM had embraced the PC?

IBM created the PC in 1981 but failed to capitalize on it. What if they had invested heavily and dominated PCs like they dominated mainframes? Would we still have Microsoft and Apple today?

Scenario B: What if mobile phones had never been invented?

Smartphones transformed society. Without them, would we have cloud computing? Would ride-sharing (Uber) exist? Would remote work be possible? Trace the cascading effects.

Scenario C: What if AI adoption stalled in 2024?

Imagine AI regulatory crackdowns or technical problems delayed AI adoption by 10 years. What industries would suffer most? Which would benefit? How would organizations adapt?

Guiding Questions:
  • ‚Ä¢ How would the alternative scenario change business?
  • ‚Ä¢ What companies would win/lose?
  • ‚Ä¢ How would jobs/careers be affected?
  • ‚Ä¢ Would society be better or worse off?
  • ‚Ä¢ What does this teach us about contingency in technology?
