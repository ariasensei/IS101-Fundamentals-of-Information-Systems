What Are IS Components?

An Information System is more than just computers and software. It's an integrated combination of five interdependent elements working together to collect, process, store, and deliver information that enables organizations to operate effectively and make better decisions.

ğŸ¯ The Foundation

Think of IS components like a car: the engine (Hardware) converts fuel into motion. The engine control system (Software) optimizes performance. The fuel tank stores the energy (Data). The driver (People) operates the car. And the driving route (Process) determines where we go. Remove any one component, and the system fails. A Ferrari with no driver won't reach anywhere. A brilliant driver in a broken car can't move. All five must work in harmony.

How Components Integrate & Work Together

The Interconnected System

The five components don't work in isolation. They're deeply interconnected. A change in one component often requires changes in others. Understanding these connections helps you see why "buying new software" alone won't fix business problems.

How the Components Connect

ğŸ”— Hardware â†” Software

Software runs ON hardware. If software requires GPU acceleration but you have old servers without GPUs, software performance suffers. Conversely, hardware without appropriate software is useless.

Example: Game developers create graphics software optimized for RTX GPUs. If your PC has an RTX 4090 (hardware) but plays a game designed for basic GPUs, you're wasting hardware potential. Software and hardware must match.

ğŸ”— Software â†” Data

Software processes and manages data. Poor data quality makes software useless (garbage in = garbage out). Good software can help maintain data quality by validating inputs and enforcing rules.

Example: Email validation software checks if email addresses follow the correct format before storing them. Bad software accepts "notanemail" and corrupts your data. Good software validates during entry.

ğŸ”— Data â†” People

People create, use, and interpret data. Without people, data just sits idle. People need training to use data properly. Bad data literacy means people make poor decisions.

Example: A sales team might misinterpret a report showing declining revenue. They blame marketing when it's actually a data entry error (January numbers entered twice). Good people understand data limitations.

ğŸ”— People â†” Process

Processes are designed BY people and executed BY people. If a process is too complicated, people will circumvent it ("shadow processes"). Good processes empower people; bad processes frustrate them.

Example: A company creates an approval process requiring 5 sign-offs for small purchases. Workers find workarounds (splitting orders, informal approvals). Simpler process = people follow it.

ğŸ”— Process â†” Hardware

Processes determine what hardware you need. A company processing 1M transactions/day needs different hardware than one processing 100 transactions/day. Process drives hardware requirements.

Example: Netflix's process of recommending shows requires processing billions of user actions. This demands massive compute power (hardware). If they didn't do recommendations, they'd need much less hardware.

Let's trace a customer ordering coffee on a mobile app to see all five components in action:

2ï¸âƒ£ App displays menu (Software)

3ï¸âƒ£ Customer selects order (Data)

4ï¸âƒ£ Payment processed (Process)

5ï¸âƒ£ Barista notified (People)

A worker (person) at the store receives the order notification on a screen (hardware) and starts making the coffee, following their procedure (process).

6ï¸âƒ£ Order ready (Feedback Loop)

Barista marks order complete. Software updates data (order status). Customer gets notification (on their hardware). Process captures feedback for improving future orders.

What Happens If One Component Fails?

âŒ Hardware Fails

Starbucks app servers crash. Even though software, data, people are ready, customers can't access the app. Orders stop.

Impact: Total system down, revenue loss, customer frustration

âŒ Software Bugs

App crashes after payment submission. Order data not saved. Good hardware and people can't help.

Impact: Corrupted transactions, refund chaos, trust erosion

âŒ Data Corrupted

Order data gets mixed up. Wrong orders sent to stores. Customers receive someone else's order.

Impact: Customer dissatisfaction, operational chaos, compliance issues

âŒ People Shortage

Orders received perfectly, but not enough baristas to make them. Queue grows for hours.

Impact: Delivery delays, customer frustration, negative reviews

âŒ Process Error

Baristas don't follow the order-taking process. Payments not validated before sending to store. Customers dispute charges.

Impact: Revenue leakage, refund costs, operational confusion

The Component Lesson

Companies with legacy on-premise infrastructure couldn't quickly pivot. The five components were tightly coupled:

  • Hardware: Servers in office buildings (couldn't bring home)
  • Software: Designed for office network access
  • Data: Security policies required VPN connection to office
  • People: Trained only for office work
  • Process: Required physical presence and signatures

Companies that had already decoupled components (cloud hardware, remote-capable software, digital data access, trained remote workers, digital processes) thrived.

ğŸ”® The Future Lesson

Companies that design components to be flexible and interchangeable survive disruptions. Tightly coupled components are fragile. COVID accelerated cloud adoption by 5 years because companies realized the value of flexible components.
